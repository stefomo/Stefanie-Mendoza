InsuLearn



Reimaging Diabetes Education

Scope: UX/UI, User Research, Web Design   Duration: Jun-Sep 2020   Team: Allison P. , Stephanie K. , & Vidya R.


Overview


Over 10 weeks my group and I, along with the help from mentors Gayle Lorenzi, RN, CDCES, Arizona Milotich, Dr. Lars Mueller, and Heidi Rataj designed an interactive visual tool for both healthcare provider and patients to support their learning of diabetes management.

Our mission was to make diabetes, specifically the relationship between insulin and glucose, easier to understand and manage. We wanted our project to focus on allowing people to see their diabetes in a clear, digestible way, and therefore focused on interactive visualizations as a learning tool.



Check out the tool here

Background


Diabetes Technology like continous glucose monitors (CGM) and Insulin Pumps makes huge amounts of data available to people with diabetes. This data allows people to understand how diabetes affects their body and understanding this data can lead to a more effective diabetes management. However, there is little support and a lack of tools to help people with diabetes understand essential diabetes concepts.





Fig. 1. Examples of CGM Data



Challenge


Living with diabetes can be stressful. On top of the physical, emotional, and financial challenges, once a person is diagnosed, there is an overwhelming amount of information they need to learn and understand quickly in order to manage a healthy lifestyle: Figuring out food intake, complex carbs, exercising, the technology, the side effects of medication, and more.

So what are the methods and tools people are using to learn and teach about diabetes?





Fig. 2. Healthcare providers explaining trends and activity.




Currently, most healthcare providers rely on pen and paper to explain insulin and glucose patterns to their patients. The patient learns in real-time but, what if they have questions? They have to book another appointment via telehealth, leaving lots of opportunity for missed education and understanding. How can we optimize these tools from paper to digital with active visualization and empower users to try it at home and on their own time?

User Research


To further understand the core problems within diabetes education, our team dove into this challenging domain by conducting research and interviews with two health care providers and four continous monitoring (CGM)/pump users to discover possible solutions for teaching and learning.

Here are the four key insights we learned:

1. Lack of Visual Learning Tools
Healthcare providers rely on impromptu drawings to explain important concepts


2. Information Overload
Patients expressed that when first approaching diabetes, simple is better

“It was so overwhelming. [During] the first appointment, it’s like I blacked out. I just barely remember sticking myself with a needle.”

-Participant T, T1D and Diabetes Educator


3. Long Trial and Error Phase
Pump users went through a long trial and error phase to understand insulin timing

“If you’re newly diagnosed, there is a lot of testing and trial and error to get to where I am. There is a steep learning curve and lots of frustrations”

-Participant M, TP1


4. Pre-Bolusing is a Challenging Concept
Pre-bolusing is a relatively new concept and people are sometimes reluctant to try it.


How might we help CGM & pump users better understand and manage insulin timing and dosing?

Ideation


The initial ideation focused on three opportunity points gathered from the user research.





Fig. 3. Rapid ideation sketches.




1. Understanding Insulin Timing and Dosing
Healthcare providers stressed understanding insulin is essential.


2. Providing Personalized Care
Adjusting relevant insights and feedback to the person personalizes their experience.


3. Using Visual Explanations
Rather than paper, simple digital interactive learning tools are better.



Although there are many concepts to teach for diabetes management, we decided to focus our tool on teaching the importance of insulin timing and dosing for glucose levels. By keeping the tool simple, we are able to hone in the key insulin concept.


Fig. 4. Early wireframe for our manually sliding graph.


We implemented an interactive insulin time and dose sliders to simulate how the individual’s action can affect their glucose levels in real time.
